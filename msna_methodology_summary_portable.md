
<h1>Sampling Methodology Summary</h1>
<h3>Multi-Sectoral Needs Assessment — North-West, North-East and North-Central Nigeria, 2026</h3>
<p>This note describes the sampling design used to select households for interview across the Non-IDP and internally displaced (IDP) populations covered by this assessment. It is written as a methodology reference, not an implementation log — it does not describe the software, data-processing steps, or tools used to build the sample, only the statistical design itself and the reasoning behind it. Sections marked <strong>Technical detail</strong> throughout are intended to eventually move into a technical annex; everything else is written for a general, non-technical reader. It is intended as a first, comprehensive draft to be trimmed and adapted into whatever format is needed for the final Terms of Reference or technical annex.</p>
<p>Population groups are defined as <strong>IDPs</strong> and <strong>Non-IDPs</strong> — not "host" — to avoid the ambiguity of a term that, in humanitarian usage, also describes the community an IDP lives embedded within (an "IDP in host community", one of the categories recorded against the IDP population below). Framing the two sampled groups as IDP / Non-IDP avoids relying on an assumption about whether returnees and refugees are or aren't part of "the host community" for sampling purposes — they are simply outside the IDP population as recorded by IOM DTM, and therefore Non-IDP.</p>
<hr>
<h2>1. Sampling frame and stratification</h2>
<p>The assessment covers Non-IDP and internally displaced (IDP) populations across three regions of northern Nigeria — North-West, North-East and North-Central — spanning fourteen states. Non-IDP and IDP populations are treated as two entirely separate sampling frames throughout the design, reflecting both the different population data available for each and, as described in Section 3, a different field procedure for each.</p>
<p>The sampling universe was built from a regular hexagonal grid covering the assessment area, rather than directly from administrative or settlement boundaries. A regular grid gives every part of the landscape an equal opportunity to be represented in the frame and avoids the irregular shapes and highly uneven sizes of using Local Government Area (LGA) or ward polygons as the base sampling unit. Two independent population layers were then attributed to this grid, one per population group:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Non-IDP population</strong> — drawn from a gridded population estimate for Nigeria, giving an estimated number of people and households in each grid cell.</li>
<li><strong>IDP population</strong> — drawn from site-level location data collected under the International Organization for Migration's Displacement Tracking Matrix (DTM), which records IDP and returnee sites together with their GPS coordinates and an estimated number of households and individuals present at each. Each recorded site's population was attributed to whichever grid cell contains it. DTM also records a <strong>Population Category</strong> for each site — returnee-classified sites are excluded from the IDP frame entirely (unchanged from the original design); every remaining IDP site is further recorded as either <strong>in-camp</strong> or <strong>in a host community</strong>, kept as a field on every IDP record for future use (see Section 3).</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Technical detail.</strong> The Non-IDP population layer is WorldPop's constrained gridded population estimate for Nigeria (100m resolution, 2026 projection, release R2025A). A grid cell — for either population layer — is retained in the sampling frame only if its estimated household count exceeds 5 (equivalent to roughly 30 people, using an assumed average household size of 6). Cells at or below this threshold are treated as having no meaningful population and are dropped before stratification; the same threshold is applied identically to the Non-IDP and IDP layers.</p>
<p>Grid cells with no meaningful population under a given layer were excluded from the corresponding frame. As a result, a Non-IDP cluster can only ever be drawn from a cell known to have a resident Non-IDP population, and an IDP cluster can only ever be drawn from a cell containing at least one recorded IDP or returnee site — there is no possibility of a cluster being assigned to an empty or unpopulated location by construction.</p>
<p>Both frames were then stratified by Admin-2 (Local Government Area) and population type together, producing up to two strata per Admin-2 — one Non-IDP, one IDP, wherever both populations are present. Each stratum was treated as an independent sampling domain with its own required sample size, its own selection of clusters, and its own design weight. Stratifying at Admin-2 level, the level the assessment reports findings against, ensures the sample is spread proportionately across administrative units rather than concentrating in a small number of high-population districts.</p>
<p>Before either population layer was gridded, a further exclusion was applied to define the accessible area available for sampling in the first place: an area along Nigeria's international borders, and a set of areas separately assessed as inaccessible, were both removed from the sampling universe entirely (see Section 4 for the exact rule).</p>

<h2>2. Stage 1: primary sampling unit selection</h2>
<p>Within each stratum, the number of clusters required was calculated using a standard two-stage cluster-survey sample size formula, combining:</p>
<ul>
<li>a 90% confidence level,</li>
<li>a conservative assumption of a 50% expected proportion for the indicators being measured (the assumption that maximises the required sample size, and so a safe default where the true proportion is not known in advance),</li>
<li>a 10% margin of error,</li>
<li>a design effect reflecting the extra imprecision introduced by clustering households together rather than sampling them fully at random, based on an assumed intra-cluster correlation coefficient and a cluster size of six households per cluster,</li>
<li>a finite-population correction using each stratum's estimated household population, and</li>
<li>a 10% buffer added to the resulting figure to allow for attrition and cluster-level non-response, before rounding up to a whole number of six-household clusters.</li>
</ul>
<p>Strata with a very small total household population — below the equivalent of six clusters' worth of households — were treated as <strong>certainty strata</strong>: every eligible grid cell in that stratum was included in the sample directly, rather than a subset being drawn, since the underlying population is too small to sample from meaningfully. All other strata used <strong>probability-proportional-to-size (PPS)</strong> sampling, in which the chance of a given grid cell being selected as a cluster is proportional to its estimated household population — the same measure of size used to build the frame in the first place (the gridded population estimate for Non-IDP strata; the DTM-reported household count for IDP strata). A more densely populated location is therefore proportionately more likely to be selected than a sparsely populated one, which keeps the achieved sample self-weighting toward where most of the population actually lives, while still giving every eligible location in the stratum a non-zero chance of selection.</p>
<p>PPS selection was implemented as a systematic sample with a random starting point: grid cells within a stratum were placed in a fixed order, a random starting point was chosen along the cumulative population total for that stratum, and clusters were then selected at fixed population intervals along that ordering. Because the interval is fixed while the population of individual grid cells varies, a single very large or densely populated cell can occasionally be selected more than once within the same stratum. Where this happened, the repeated selections were combined into a single cluster carrying a proportionally larger household quota, rather than being treated as two separate cluster visits to the same physical location.</p>
<p>A fixed random seed was used for this Stage 1 selection, so that, given the same input population data, the exact set of selected clusters can be reproduced — an important property for an assessment that may need to demonstrate its sample was drawn consistently and without manual interference.</p>

<h2>3. Stage 2: household selection procedure</h2>
<p>Stage 2 — selecting which specific households within a chosen cluster are actually approached for interview — was designed and implemented differently for Non-IDP and IDP clusters. This reflects a genuine, deliberate difference in what data is available and reliable for identifying individual households in each context, and is the single most important operational distinction for field teams to understand (see the companion field guide for how this translates into instructions for enumerators).</p>
<h3>Non-IDP clusters: building-footprint household selection</h3>
<p>For Non-IDP clusters, individual building footprints identified from high-resolution satellite imagery were used as a proxy for household locations within the selected grid cell. Footprints were filtered to a minimum detection-confidence threshold and to a plausible residential size range, which excludes both very small non-residential fragments — such as outbuildings, latrines, or storage structures frequently mapped as separate footprints within a single household compound — and unusually large structures unlikely to house a single household. From the resulting set of eligible building locations within a cluster, six households were selected at random, without replacement, as the primary sample, with a further set of up to six additional households drawn and ranked in order as a reserve list. Field teams use this reserve list strictly in rank order whenever a primary household cannot be reached or declines to participate.</p>
<p><strong>Technical detail.</strong> A building footprint is eligible if its detection confidence score is at least 0.75 and its footprint area is between 12m² and 1,000m². The lower bound excludes small non-residential fragments (outbuildings, latrines, storage structures); the upper bound excludes very large structures — warehouses, markets, institutional buildings — unlikely to be a single household's residence.</p>

<p>A small number of selected grid cells contained no building footprint meeting the eligibility criteria at all — typically very sparsely built-up or marginal locations. Rather than leaving these clusters empty, each was reallocated to a different, previously unselected grid cell within the same stratum, drawn using the identical population-proportional selection method as the original Stage 1 draw. This preserves the statistical integrity of the design — the replacement cluster's chance of selection is calculated exactly as any other cluster's would be — while ensuring every cluster in the final sample corresponds to a real, visitable location with an actual population of eligible households to draw from.</p>
<h3>IDP clusters: site-based household selection</h3>
<p>The original design intended to use the same building-footprint method for IDP clusters. This was found, during the course of building the sample, not to work well for IDP locations specifically, for two related reasons.</p>
<p>First, a practical constraint: IDP locations exist in the sampling frame only where they have actually been recorded as populated in the underlying site-level data. Unlike Non-IDP areas, there is no large surrounding pool of unselected grid cells to draw on if a given site's satellite-derived building footprints prove unreliable or unavailable — checking this directly found that, in the large majority of affected districts, every eligible IDP location had already been drawn into the sample, leaving no possibility of a like-for-like substitute of the kind used for Non-IDP clusters.</p>
<p>Second, a data-quality consideration: satellite-derived building footprints are a weaker proxy for individual households in many IDP and informal-settlement contexts than in typical Non-IDP neighbourhoods, since shelter arrangements in such settings can be denser, more temporary, or simply not correspond one-to-one with distinct visible structures from overhead imagery.</p>
<p>For these reasons, the household selection method for IDP clusters uses the site's own recorded GPS location directly, rather than satellite-derived building footprints. Every selected IDP cluster corresponds to a site recorded in the DTM dataset, so a real, field-verifiable location is always available by construction. A fixed radius of <strong>150 metres</strong> around that recorded point was defined as the boundary of "the site" for field purposes. This distance was set after checking how closely spaced known IDP and returnee sites are to one another nationally, so that it is wide enough to comfortably cover a typical site's extent while remaining unlikely to spill into a neighbouring, distinct site recorded separately in the same area. In a minority of cases, a single selected grid cell corresponded to more than one recorded site nearby; in those cases, the larger site by reported household count was used as the cluster's location, with the presence of the smaller, nearby site retained as supplementary information rather than discarded.</p>
<p>Every IDP record also now carries the site's DTM <strong>Population Category</strong> group — in-camp or in a host community (Section 1) — recorded from the start of the sample build so it is available for any future field procedure that treats the two differently; the 150-metre single-radius method described here is, for now, applied uniformly to both.</p>
<p>*[Map: Example IDP cluster — hexagon boundary, the site's GPS point, and the 150m field radius. See output/methodology_map_idp_site.png.]*</p>
<p>*[Map: Close-up of the 150m site radius around the IDP site GPS point. See output/methodology_map_idp_site_closeup.png.]*</p>
<p>Under this method, field teams are given the site's GPS point, the 150-metre radius, and a target number of interviews — six primary plus up to six reserve, matching the Non-IDP protocol — and are responsible for constructing a short household list covering that site on arrival, then using a random selection procedure to choose which households on that list are interviewed. This is standard practice for camp- or site-based population assessments, where the population actually present at a location can shift over time, making a list built at the point of data collection more reliable than one derived in advance from remote imagery.</p>
<p>Both approaches produce the same underlying record structure — one row per planned interview, primary or reserve, each carrying a design weight and full administrative attribution down to ward level — so that Non-IDP and IDP records can be analysed together as a single dataset despite having been generated through two different field procedures.</p>

<h2>4. Known limitations and edge cases</h2>
<ul>
<li><p><strong>Certainty strata and the exclusion rule.</strong> A small number of strata — those with the smallest total household population — are eligible to be sampled with certainty (every eligible location included) rather than through probability selection, because the underlying population is too small to draw a meaningful sample from. Since a 2026-07-23 revision, a certainty stratum is only retained in the design if full enumeration — every eligible site, still capped at six households each — can reach the assessment's 10% margin-of-error target; where it can't, the stratum is excluded from the sample entirely (zero clusters, zero interviews) rather than fielded for indicative-only data, since HQ judged that trade-off between operational effort and reporting value not worth making. Under the current population data, all 18 strata that would otherwise be certainty strata fail this check and are excluded — see Section 5 for the rule, the affected LGAs, and the resulting coverage gap.</p>
</li>
<li><p><strong>Repeated cluster selection.</strong> In strata where population is concentrated in a small number of very large grid cells, the same cell can be selected more than once by the systematic PPS procedure. Where this occurred, it was treated as a single cluster with a proportionally larger household quota rather than two separate field visits — the methodologically correct way to preserve each unit's true probability of selection under this design.</p>
</li>
<li><p><strong>Zero-eligible-location clusters (Non-IDP).</strong> A small number of Non-IDP clusters had no eligible building footprint at all and were reallocated to a different location within the same stratum, using the same selection method as the original draw (see Section 3). This does not affect the overall stratum sample size or the statistical properties of the design; it only affects which specific location represents that cluster's "slot" in the sample.</p>
</li>
<li><p><strong>Below-target clusters.</strong> A cluster is flagged as <strong>below-target</strong> (<code>below_target_cluster</code> in the dataset) when fewer eligible households were available in it than its interview target. The consequence of this flag differs by population group, reflecting the different Stage 2 methods (Section 3). For <strong>Non-IDP</strong> clusters, the number of eligible building footprints genuinely limits how many households can be drawn — a below-target Non-IDP cluster's contribution to the sample is capped at whatever was actually available, and it delivers fewer than its full target. For <strong>IDP</strong> clusters, there is no such cap: every IDP cluster's full target number of interview slots is always planned, regardless of the DTM-reported household count at that site, since the actual household list is built by field teams on arrival rather than drawn from a pre-existing pool (Section 3). A below-target IDP cluster therefore still plans its full target — the flag is a heads-up that the DTM estimate on file is lower than that target, worth field-team awareness, not a reduction in planned interviews. In total, 85 clusters out of 5,779 (across both Non-IDP and IDP) are flagged, and are retained in the dataset so they can be reviewed as needed.</p>
</li>
<li><p><strong>Non-IDP strata with a below-target shortfall: identified, corrected.</strong> For every Non-IDP stratum, the calculated target sample size (Section 2) is at least 96 households — the threshold this assessment's validation process treats as a general minimum for stratum-level representativeness. In 28 of the 323 Non-IDP strata, enough of that stratum's clusters were individually below-target that the <em>delivered</em> sample fell short of that target, despite the target itself being set correctly. These 28 span nine states (Benue, Borno, Kogi, Nasarawa, Niger, Plateau, Sokoto, Yobe, Zamfara), most concentrated in — but not limited to — Borno's conflict-affected North-East LGAs (12 of the 28). The most plausible explanation for the concentration in Borno is that satellite-derived building footprints undercount real households in specific conflict-affected areas because structures have been destroyed, abandoned, or not rebuilt since displacement — the same underlying data-quality limitation already noted for IDP sites above; the remaining, more geographically scattered strata reflect the same building-footprint-availability limitation in isolated locations elsewhere.</p>
<p>Recalculating the realized margin of error for each of these 28 strata — using the same formula, design effect, and intra-cluster correlation assumption as the original sample-size calculation, but evaluated at the sample actually achieved rather than the sample targeted — found that only 2 of the 28 (Mafa and Ngala) genuinely exceeded the study's 10% margin-of-error threshold before correction; the other 26 were already within tolerance (9.31–9.98%). Rather than apply a uniform fix to a fixed list, every Non-IDP stratum is checked against this same rule (achieved sample below target_sample), and each one that fails it gets the minimum number of brand-new supplementary clusters needed to close its own specific gap — drawn using the identical population-proportional method as every other cluster in the design (see Section 2), at the standard six households per cluster rather than a larger one. Cluster selection probabilities were recalculated for every hexagon in any stratum that received an added cluster, since adding a cluster changes that stratum's effective number of Stage-1 draws; this keeps design weights consistent with the actual number of clusters visited.</p>
<p>This replaces an earlier approach (superseded 2026-07-22) that raised the cluster size from six to seven households for a hand-picked list of 10 conflict-affected LGAs — verified at the time to add 421 additional interviews for those 10 alone. The current approach identifies a broader, more consistently-defined set of 28 strata using the same target-vs-achieved check throughout, and closes all of them for 254 total additional interviews combined. The resulting realized MoE (9.03–9.27% across all 28) sits closer to — while still safely under — the 10% design ceiling than the previous fix's 8.51–8.73% for its 10 LGAs; that gap reflects the previous approach's over-correction (a seven-household cluster size delivered more precision than the design required, at a larger field-burden cost), not a change in what "safely under 10%" means.</p>

<p>All 28 strata are now at or above their target_sample, converging to a tight, consistent realized MoE range (9.03–9.27%) regardless of how far short of target each one started — each needed between 6 and 8 additional interviews (1–2 supplementary clusters) to close its own specific gap; none needed a large correction, consistent with the minimal-correction design of this mechanism. The intra-cluster correlation coefficient (ICC = 0.06) and cluster size (m = 6) are unchanged from the standard design throughout — only the achieved sample itself changed, via added clusters rather than any change to an underlying assumption. The margin-of-error figures shown incorporate the finite-population correction from each LGA's true household population, consistent with the original sample-size formula.</p>
<p>For context, a separate and much larger group — 124 of the assessment's 242 IDP strata — also deliver fewer than 96 households, but for an entirely different reason that does not indicate a shortfall: the calculated target itself is below 96 in those strata, because the DTM-recorded IDP population within that specific LGA is genuinely small. Unlike the 28 Non-IDP strata above, nothing was "lost" between target and delivery in these IDP strata — the design intentionally scales the sample to the population actually recorded there rather than imposing a fixed minimum that would oversample small IDP populations relative to their true size.</p>
</li>
<li><p><strong>Border-buffer and inaccessible-area exclusions.</strong> Two separate rules define the accessible sampling universe, both developed in consultation with FACT Foundation, the assessment's operational partner responsible for field team coordination. First, a buffer zone was excluded along Nigeria's international borders with all four neighbouring countries: 20km along the border with Niger, and 5km along the borders with Chad, Cameroon and Benin. Second, a set of Admin-3 areas within the North-East region, separately assessed by FACT as either inaccessible or not currently existing as functioning settlements, were excluded entirely. This second, assessment-based exclusion is currently available for the North-East only — in North-Central and North-West, the border buffer is the only exclusion applied, and no equivalent field-verified accessibility assessment has yet been incorporated for those two regions.</p>
</li>
<li><p><strong>Administrative boundary coverage.</strong> The nationally standard ward-level (Admin-3) administrative boundary dataset is only officially available for the North-East region. A secondary, nationally complete ward boundary source was used as the primary basis for ward-level attribution across all three regions, with the official dataset retained as a secondary cross-reference in the North-East specifically.</p>
</li>
<li><p><strong>Source-tile boundary handling (Non-IDP).</strong> The satellite-derived building footprint dataset is delivered as several large geographic tiles that overlap slightly at their edges, and each tile is itself processed in smaller pieces. A cluster whose eligible buildings happened to fall across more than one of these pieces was, during quality assurance, found in a subset of cases to have its eligible-building pool and Stage 2 selection probability calculated from an incomplete portion of its true buildings. This was identified and corrected prior to finalising the sample: every cluster's eligible-building pool is now consolidated across all relevant source pieces before households are drawn and weights are calculated, and the corrected frame was independently re-verified, building by building, before delivery.</p>
</li>
<li><p><strong>Provisional IDP household counts.</strong> The number of households used to calculate the within-cluster selection probability for IDP clusters is currently the figure reported in the DTM dataset at the time the sample was drawn, not a count confirmed by field teams on the ground. This is addressed directly in the weighting section below.</p>
</li>
</ul>

<h2>5. Excluded strata: the certainty-stratum coverage gap</h2>
<p>As of a 2026-07-23 revision, 18 LGAs' IDP populations are excluded from the sample entirely: zero clusters, zero interviews planned. This is a deliberate HQ-approved design decision, not a data-quality issue or an execution shortfall, and is documented here on its own — separately from the general limitations list — given its direct, LGA-specific implication for what can and cannot be reported.</p>
<p><strong>Why this happens.</strong> A stratum is eligible to be treated as certainty (Section 2) when its total household population falls below the equivalent of six clusters at the standard household-per-cluster target — 36 households — since every eligible site is included rather than a probability sample being drawn from a population too small to sample from meaningfully. Stage 2 still caps interviews at six per site rather than fully enumerating every household, and — unlike a probability (PPS) stratum falling short of its target — a certainty stratum has no supplementary-cluster mechanism available to close the gap (Section 4's "identified, corrected" fix only applies to PPS strata): every eligible site is already included, so there is nowhere left to draw an additional cluster from. Where even that full-enumeration ceiling can't resolve to the assessment's 10% margin of error, the underlying population is judged too small, relative to the assessment's precision target, for a field visit to produce anything better than indicative data. Previously (before 2026-07-23) such strata were still fielded and their findings flagged as indicative; HQ has since decided the trade-off between the field effort of visiting these sites and the reporting value of indicative-only data is not worth it, and these strata are now excluded from data collection outright rather than visited.</p>
<p><strong>The rule.</strong> For every certainty-eligible stratum, the margin of error that full enumeration would deliver is projected — using the identical formula, design effect, and intra-cluster correlation assumption used throughout this document, evaluated at the maximum achievable sample (every eligible site × six households). Where that projected figure exceeds 10%, the stratum is excluded before Stage 1 cluster selection runs: none of its sites are drawn, so it contributes no clusters and no interviews to the delivered sample. This check is fully generic, not a hand-picked list — it is re-evaluated from each stratum's own population figures every time the sample is built, so a future IOM DTM data refresh that grows the recorded IDP population in any of these 18 LGAs enough to pass the check would make that LGA eligible again (as either a certainty or a probability stratum) without any change to the design itself.</p>
<p>Applying this rule to the current population data finds that <strong>all 18</strong> of the LGAs that would otherwise have been certainty strata fail it — every one of today's certainty-eligible IDP strata is excluded; none remain in the achieved sample. All 18 are IDP strata (there are no Non-IDP certainty strata in this design), spanning four states: 14 in Kano, 2 in Niger, and 1 each in Kaduna and Kebbi.</p>

<p><strong>Coverage by state.</strong> The practical effect varies considerably by state, and is most severe for Kebbi: Gwandu was Kebbi's <em>only</em> IDP stratum, so its exclusion leaves <strong>Kebbi State with no IDP interviews planned at all</strong> under the current design — no LGA-, state-, or regional-level IDP estimate is possible for Kebbi from this sample. The other three affected states retain partial IDP coverage: Kano retains 16 of its 30 IDP LGAs (14 excluded), Niger retains 11 of its 13 (2 excluded), and Kaduna retains 21 of its 22 (1 excluded) — all as active certainty or PPS strata unaffected by this rule. <p><strong>Reporting guidance.</strong> For the 18 excluded LGAs, no IDP indicator of any kind can be reported — not even as indicative — because no data was collected there; this is a coverage gap, not a precision caveat, and should be described as such (e.g. "IDP population not directly assessed" rather than "estimate based on a small sample"). State- and regional-level IDP aggregates for Kano, Niger, and Kaduna are based only on their remaining, actively-sampled IDP strata and implicitly exclude the DTM-recorded population of the excluded LGAs within them — see Section 6 for the corresponding weighting implication. No IDP aggregate of any kind is possible for Kebbi State. Analysts producing state- or regional-level IDP totals should note this gap explicitly rather than let it pass silently as if the full DTM-recorded IDP population were represented.</p>

<h2>6. Weighting approach</h2>
<p>The design weight for each planned interview reflects both stages of selection, and is calculated as the inverse of the overall probability that the household would be selected: one divided by the probability of its cluster being selected, multiplied by the probability of the household being selected within that cluster.</p>
<p>The <strong>cluster-level (Stage 1) selection probability</strong> is 1 for every cluster in a certainty stratum, since every eligible location in that stratum was included with certainty. In PPS strata, it is proportional to the cluster's estimated household population relative to the total estimated household population of the stratum, scaled by the number of clusters drawn from that stratum — consistent with the population-proportional selection method described in Section 2.</p>
<p>The <strong>household-level (Stage 2) selection probability</strong> is the ratio of the number of households targeted for interview in that cluster (six, or fewer for a below-target cluster) to the total number of eligible households identified within it — the count of eligible building footprints for Non-IDP clusters, or the DTM-reported household count for IDP clusters.</p>
<p>The resulting design weight should be applied in any survey-weighted analysis of the collected data, together with the stratum (Admin-2 by population type, <code>strata_id</code>) and cluster identifiers, so that estimates and their confidence intervals correctly account for the clustered, stratified, unequal-probability nature of the design, rather than treating the collected interviews as a simple random sample.</p>
<p>Because both selection probabilities can vary substantially across clusters — population density varies widely between a dense urban grid cell and a sparse rural one, and a fixed six-household target is drawn against that varying pool — the resulting design weights span a wide range, from 1 (a certainty-stratum or fully-enumerated small cluster) up to several thousand for the largest, most densely built clusters in PPS strata. This is an expected consequence of the design rather than an error, but it does imply a non-trivial design effect: analysts should expect the effective sample size for weighted indicator estimates to be smaller than the nominal number of completed interviews, and should check the realised design effect once data collection is complete.</p>
<p><strong>One point requires attention before weights are finalised for analysis.</strong> For IDP clusters, the household count used in the Stage 2 selection probability is currently the DTM's own reported estimate for that site, not a count confirmed on the ground. Once field teams report the actual number of households present at each IDP site at the time of listing, the Stage 2 selection probability — and therefore the final design weight — for IDP interviews should be recalculated using that confirmed figure in place of the DTM estimate, before any weighted analysis of IDP data is treated as final.</p>
<p><strong>Coverage gap from excluded strata (Section 5).</strong> The 18 excluded IDP strata contribute no records at all to the dataset, so there are no design weights to apply for them — they are simply absent, not present with a large or unstable weight. Any weighted state- or regional-level IDP total is therefore a total over the DTM-recorded IDP population in the <em>sampled</em> strata only, not the full DTM-recorded IDP population of that state or region; it will not sum to the latter, and the gap is not recoverable by any weighting adjustment, since no interviews exist to weight up. This is a genuine coverage gap rather than a weighting artefact, and should be stated explicitly wherever a weighted IDP aggregate is reported for Kano, Niger, Kaduna, or (where no aggregate is possible at all) Kebbi.</p>
